# Scheduled Iteration Context

Work on the existing game at games/ashes-of-rome. Do not create a new game.

Read README.md, DEVELOPMENT.md, LEGAL.md, docs/loom-writing.md, story.yaml, every chapter file, and recent commits before changing anything.

This is a long-form historical interactive-fiction project built on Loom. The player begins mortal in Rome. Economic position, work, education, relationships, politics, religion, art, and behavior are established through play. Supernatural relationships should emerge from the player's lived background and choices rather than a direct selection menu.

The long-term arc crosses multiple historical eras and reaches modern nights. Historical jumps are valuable when they transform earlier consequences. Build durable threads that can evolve across time: people into families and institutions; favors into obligations and customs; lies into rumors and doctrine; enemies into networks and factions; objects into inheritance and disputed relics; places into institutions, ruins, and redevelopments.

Torpor is a narrative editing tool. It can make the player miss events, wake too early or late, and discover that remembered people and choices have been distorted.

Keep real history structurally important. Supernatural politics can exploit, survive, distort, and react to history, but should not become the secret cause of every human event.

Player feedback recorded in DEVELOPMENT.md outranks autonomous plans.

For each pass: inspect briefly, choose a concrete playable target, implement it, test what is testable, and update DEVELOPMENT.md. Do not spend a pass only diagnosing, planning, or protecting hypothetical future possibilities. Later chapters should become playable as soon as earlier choices can produce interesting consequences there.

Never tell the player what the story is saving for. Avoid phrases such as "not yet," "later you will," "this is only the beginning," or authorial explanations of pacing. If a scene is tempted to promise future excitement, make the current scene more interesting instead. The game should not discuss its own structure, future transformations, or withheld character options.

You may modify the local Loom engine when the story demonstrates a concrete need. Prefer small, understandable extensions. Document engine changes and preserve static hosting, mobile usability, client-side saves, and the hackable spirit of Loom.

Avoid generic AI prose, vague prophecy language, and history-textbook exposition. Use concrete people, material pressures, places, jobs, institutions, food, rent, worship, patronage, travel, and social obligations. The story should earn emotional stakes through scenes rather than explaining its design.

Keep public repository content broadly PG-13. Dark horror and vampire subject matter are welcome, but avoid overt sexual depictions, gratuitous gore, hateful caricature, or using real historical suffering as decorative supernatural lore.

"Release readiness" means only practical fan-project hygiene: follow the current Dark Pack/fan-content requirements, keep the release checklist current, and verify required branding and notice language before publication. Do not spend development passes on contract law, legal procedure, licensing theory, or copyright essays unless a concrete release blocker is discovered.
